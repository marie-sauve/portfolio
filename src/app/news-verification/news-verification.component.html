News Verification

At a time where AI has infiltrated our daily lives and video clips go viral within seconds, being able to tell the truth from the lie has more than ever become a crucial part of the media landscape.

News verification journalists are half reporters and half sleuths, analysing every piece of information to meticulously establish a neutral portrait of a particular event.

For everything that happens in the world today, there will be someone recording it on a phone or a camera.

My job as a verification expert is to find this footage, clear or license it with its author, and verify its authenticity. I run a background check on the source, corroborate through cross-reference the date of recording, and I geolocate the images. When I present newsrooms with my report of the event, video proof in hand, the client knows that the footage has been verified as authentic and can be used online and in broadcast.

Here are a few examples of my recent work:

Gas explosion in Wilmington, MC

At Storyful, we always want to get as close to the story as possible. When it came to an explosive car crash in North Carolina recently, a dashcam gave a front-row seat. The incident happened in August, when a driver plowed into an under-construction veterinary clinic in North Carolina, and ruptured a gas line, causing a massive explosion. While we had other videos showing the incident from a distance (see below), I was convinced there had to be someone closer to the scene. I kept digging till I found this dashcam footage from a nearby-parked vehicle, which had captured the moment of the explosion.

I immediately reached out to the owner of the camera via social media, but also obtained his phone number and email address. I was able to contact him within minutes, and get his witness account, as well permission to use the dramatic visuals.

Rafah camp attack, Gaza

Social media reporting has increasingly transformed reporting on conflicts. But it presents verification challenges when using remote sources. An example of the techniques I’ve used to close this reporting gap can be seen in an incident from Gaza in 2024, when I received footage from a source on the ground, showing the aftermath of a strike at a Rafah camp that had killed dozens of civilians overnight.

The Palestinian Red Crescent Society said that the casualties, civilians who were displaced, were “trapped under the flames and in the tents destroyed by the bombardment”.

Clearly, this was a sensitive story, and needed to be carefully verified. I found matching footage that enabled me to confirm the location (see below). I was then able to use this independently confirmed information in my report on the incident.

I then wrote a copy of events, which was published on our Newswire.

The Times used this footage in an article called “Seven days in Rafah explained in maps, graphics and pictures.” The article used my verification.

A colleague contributed to this article and used the geolocation I had worked on.

Hit and run driver crash in New York, NY

On September 1, 2024, a report came out that a driver who was allegedly fleeing the scene of a hit-and-run lost control of his minivan and crashed into two vehicles in Brooklyn, New York. CBS issued a video package which comprised two pieces of footage: CCTV of the crash itself, and a cell phone angle of the aftermath.

I was able to quickly geolocate the scene of the accident and ran the address into a tracking tool to obtain the name of the occupant. This enabled me to find potential phone numbers for the source. I established contact with the owner of the camera, who confirmed that the device was his, and I secured a licensing agreement with him.

I then wrote a copy for the report that was published alongside the footage on Storyful’s Newswire.

Carjacking in Jackson, MS

Tracking down the right sources and witnesses is a huge part of what I do at Storyful. Getting close to the right people, getting their side, their videos and images, powers compelling storytelling. In May, I reported on the story of a family in West Jackson, MS, who were held at gunpoint by a suspect attempting to carjack them.

The source was named in the report. I ran this name in a tracking tool alongside the location, to obtain potential addresses. This enabled me to geo the exact address, as seen in the footage.

I also found a potential match for the source on social media platforms. From there, I was able to track down one of her children, who had posted the original footage on his Facebook account. This confirmed that I had the correct source.

From there, I was able to get the source on the phone, and get them to send me video of the harrowing ordeal, and, as importantly, grant permission for its use in covering the story.

Fire in Harrisburg, PA

Speed is often of the essence when chasing a story, or a key video. In April last year, I was reporting on a local story of a fire in Harrisburg, PA.

The source was not named, but the location was described in the article as the 600 block of Ross Street in Harrisburg. I was able to find the property where the fire occurred using Google Street Views, and to establish which neighbouring house provided the Ring camera footage.

I ran the address into a tracking tool and obtained the name of the occupants, as well as potential phone numbers. I established contact with the owner of the camera and secured an exclusive licensing agreement within the hour. The story was published onto our Newswire.
